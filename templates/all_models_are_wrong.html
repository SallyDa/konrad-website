Model approximations

Models are approximations of real systems. By definition, they are not reality, but if they include good descriptions of the most important parts of the system, they can be useful.

"All models are wrong but some are useful" George Box (statistician), 1970s.

Konrad contains lots of approximations. One assumption we make is that there are no clouds. Clouds and changes in clouds can affect the atmospheric temperature profile and how the atmosphere reacts to changes in CO2 or other factors.

The radiative transfer scheme in konrad also contains many approximations. It was designed for the current climate of Earth. When we remove all of the ozone in konrad, this drastically changes the temperature profile and under these conditions the radiation scheme has large errors.

Konrad only models the tropical atmosphere and does not include heat transport from the tropics to mid or high latitudes. Instead, so that the atmosphere in konrad does not get too hot, we reduce the amount of incoming solar radiation. In this way, we assume that all the heat transport occurs in the atmosphere, while in reality some of the heat is transported by ocean currents.

Konrad contains information about the amounts of different gasses contained in the atmosphere. The three most important gases seen by the radiation are water vapour, CO2 and ozone. CO2 is well-mixed, which means that the concentration is almost constant throughout the whole atmosphere. The concentration of CO2 is well-known, because it is measured throughout the world. The ozone profile is also well-known, so the assumptions about CO2 and ozone are good. On the other hand, water vapour amounts vary throughout the tropics and with the time of day, as water at the surface evaporates and water vapour in the atmosphere condenses and it rains. These processes are not included in konrad, where we assume that one profile of water vapour represents the whole of the tropics.
